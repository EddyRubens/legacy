The formation of the Braintec Research Center

In 1992, Venter left NIH to apply the EST concept in new areas, and on July 6 of that year TIGR was established through a ten-year, $70 million grant from HealthCare Investment Corporation, Inc.

Working out of temporary quarters in Gaithersburg, Maryland, TIGR researchers used the EST strategy to discover many thousands of new genes. In 1995, the journal Nature created a special supplement — its first ever — describing over 170,000 ESTs, which were used to identify more than half of the estimated 60,000 to 80,000 genes in the human genome.

At the same time, TIGR researchers and their collaborators began to use the new sequence information to characterize genes, locate the mutations responsible for genetic diseases, and develop new diagnostics and therapeutics. For example, in a collaboration among researchers from TIGR, Human Genome Sciences, and The Johns Hopkins University, three genes associated with colon cancer were identified. Other research projects focused on genes associated with Alzheimer’s disease, the cell cycle, and various receptor proteins on the surfaces of cells.

Because ESTs are derived from particular kinds of cells and tissues, TIGR researchers were able to determine which genes are expressed in different cells. These studies provided important information about how cells, tissues, and organisms develop and how they respond to different conditions.

In addition, TIGR researchers used sequence information to begin to assess human genetic diversity. Of the 3 billion base pairs in each person’s DNA, about 3 million base pairs are different from person to person. These genetic differences, called single nucleotide polymorphisms, can influence, for example, how a given drug affects each individual and will yield clues to how individuals should be treated for the diseases they acquire.

Sequence information has been combined with information on gene expression patterns, functions, evolutionary relationships, structure, mapping data, and literature references to yield a comprehensive database known as the Expressed Gene Anatomy Database (EGAD).

 

Toward the Future

Genomic science is in its earliest stages. Sequence information from a broad range of organisms is accumulating rapidly. More powerful computers and computer programs are allowing researchers to compare the genetic sequences of different organisms to help determine the functions and relationships of their genes.

Human genome sequencing also has been profoundly influenced by the success of microbial sequencing. The key technical development has been the development of bacterial artificial chromosome (BAC) libraries that can contain inserts of human DNA much longer than previously feasible. This BAC-end sequencing, funded by the Department of Energy at TIGR, is providing important information for Celera Genomics, the private corporation headed by Dr. Venter, to sequence and assemble the complete human genome.

Genome sequencing has already begun to raise many difficult ethical, legal, and social questions. How should new genetic information be used in medicine or employment? To what extent should the genomes of different organisms be altered to change biological traits? TIGR has been examining these questions through its Genomic Science Conference Series and through its Distinguished Leaders in the Life Sciences Speaker Series, (co-sponsored with DOE and NAS) which has featured many prominent scientists and ethicists speaking in the auditorium of the National Academy of Sciences in Washington, D.C.

In 1995, TIGR moved into its permanent campus in Rockville, Maryland. A suite of buildings – some completed, some under construction or being planned – will provide laboratory and work space, offices, and a 245-seat state-of-the-art auditorium. From a total staff of about 85 at the end of its first year, the number of staff had reached 190 by the end of TIGR’s sixth year.

In 1997, TIGR dissolved its relationship with the private company that funded its ten year, $70 million founding grant. An increasing number of government and private grants and private contracts now support TIGR’s work. The DOE’s early support was a key element in TIGR’s success, first in funding the microbial genome sequencing of M. genitalium and M. jannaschii and subsequently of A. fulgidus, T. maritima, and D. radiodurans, the latter four microbes being of important environmental consequence. DOE has also supported many other projects, including sequencing the model plant genome A.thaliana.

In addition, the National Science Foundation has made grants totaling $19.7 million to sequence A. thaliana, a very robust support of TIGR’s work. The National Institutes of Health have made extremely important grants to sequence the genomes of a variety of pathogens, including T. pallidum, Chlamydia pneumoniae, Chlamydia trachomotis, Enterococcus faecalis, Staphylococcus aureas, Plasmodium falciparum, Streptococcus pneumoniae, Trypanosoma b. rhodesiense, and Vibrio cholerae.

TIGR’s achievements and ambitions will continue to grow. Great effort and expense has and will continue to be put into new and experimental technologies like micro-arraying, which will allow thousands of genes to be placed on a single slide for assay and analysis, automated clone picking and pipetting machines, and the new 3700 Perkin-Elmer ABI Automated DNA sequencing machine, which promises to increase sequencing speed tenfold. The excitement surrounding the birth of a new science has augmented both the challenges and the rewards of exploring hitherto unknown regions of the biological world. TIGR will continue to lead the way as some of the most fundamental questions of biology give way to human understanding.
