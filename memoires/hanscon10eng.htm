The hotel that we had chosen at the Tourist Information isn’t looking good. It is empty, boring. Wouldn’t there be something else to find? Close to it there is a nice looking exposition space. We go in. Good art of young Japanese artists. We chat with the owner, who can fortunately make himself clear in English. We ask him if he knows a better hotel. Oh yes he does. In the neighborhood is a guesthouse where a lot of artists stay when they want to spent some time in the center of Tokyo. That isn’t so rich but it is cozy and a part of the artists speak English. Heather and I both buy a small art object in his gallery. The credit cards work well.

On our way to the guesthouse. Heather thinks we are being followed. I have that feeling as well, since we left that recommended hotel. We follow a slalom route for a while and separate shortly and then look back. It is clear now that someone is following us. That can’t be FBI or Japanese police because they can follow us through our credit cards if they want to. It has to be Braintec! How do they know where we are? The following started at the hotel. Were they waiting for us there? Heather says that the Japanese from the Tourist Information at the airport recommended this hotel very explicitly. Should she be part of Braintec?

We decide to get rid of this follower before we reach the guesthouse. Through a department store we manage. The guesthouse is far behind at the highest floor above a gallery. There is space enough, so we can get in despite the fact we’re not artists. It exists of a couple of ateliers, a couple of sleeping rooms and a shared living room and kitchen. The artists ask immediately if we can make contact in the United States and in the Netherlands. They like to expose abroad. We promise to do our best when we get home.

How long shall it take before Braintec finds us again? If we go for the most known tourist attractions they will certainly find us. We think it is a nice sport to trace the followers and to get rid of them again. We won’t let them involve in our program and are going to enjoy Japan. What do we surely want to see? I name the temple of the Golden Pavilion and Mount Fuji. That turns out to be Heather’s favorites as well. Today we only view the gallery beneath the guesthouse. One of the artists wants to cook for us. We say that we will pay for the groceries. Together with him we go shopping. That’s how we get to see how a Japanese supermarket looks like. It looks tasty, but no sake for me. Fortunately there is also normal beer in Japan. The meal tastes excellent. The conversation was interesting but slow. From the five Japanese people at the table one spoke English well and one a bit. That was constantly translating. With their information we can fill a couple of weeks vacation around Tokyo.

Later that night another Japanese comes in. He has a moonlight as a cleaner. They don’t know about a WIK (Dutch payment for artists) in Japan. The hotel we are going to is one of the buildings he cleans. He has heard that lately some belongings were brought to one of the hotel rooms. The hotel owner had said him to prepare that room separately. Heather and I suggest that’s a Braintec action for us.
