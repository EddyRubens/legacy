My first presentation went really well. Jeanine had organized a little corner right by the entrance of the university. I had fun brightening it up with some Braintec flags. I put up a small table with a computer and the brochures Patrick made for me. It looked good. I enjoyed showing it to people and I think my enthusiasm was contagious. A lot of people showed an interest and some even applied. Let’s hope that one of them will go through with it and become a test subject.

My intro talk is set on auto pilot now, but still feels fresh. “Would you like me to give you a short introduction to Braintec, or would you prefer to look around on your own?” Depending on their answer I then continue or let them go ahead. When I continue it goes like this: “Braintec is an American company that researches the re-experiencing of memories. This is our research center and this is the director.” I then show them the brochure and point to the pictures. “For our research we use two types of brain implant. Test subjects who get a small implant, the MSI, can only re-experience memories of other people. While with the larger implant, the MESI, it’s also possible to copy memories from the brain and upload them in small plates, which are called M-stores.” People then gaze at the pictures and soon stare at me in utter amazement. “Braintec is not organizing this research to entertain the test subjects – although I can assure you it is an amazing experience – but to gain more insight into how the brain processes memories, because we still know very little about that. If we know how our brains store information and especially how it’s held, we may possibly develop a cure for Alzheimer’s or dementia.”

That’s usually the point when all the questions come. ”Yes, but isn’t that dangerous?”  “How does it work then?” ”Where do they put the implant?” People got a kick out of looking at my bald spot with its scars and imagine the implant in my head. But the idea of actually getting one is a bit frightening to them and stops them from checking it out further. But if I then explain there’s a free trip to Portland thrown in and that they can earn $5240, - if they stay there for four weeks, it suddenly becomes a lot more attractive. Some people told me that they find their own memories are more than enough to deal with and somebody even asked me if it’s possible to remove bad memories from the brain. That woman found it very disappointing to hear that “we only work with good memories and do not provide therapeutic treatments.”

Anyway, the presentation job is fun. Jeanine came by a couple of times to bring me a cup of coffee. What a sweetie! She smiled when she saw I was having such a good time. “You look awful though. A real bimbo.” she joked. She’s right, of course, I look like a stewardess in my Braintec outfit. But it’s important that I look representative.
