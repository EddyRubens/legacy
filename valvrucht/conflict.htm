INTO CONFLICT
 


Conflict is waarschijnlijk de langstbestaande en meest bekende punkband. Ondanks de vele geruchten omtrent hen, staan het “kommando” nog stevig. In feite: de muziek die Conflict nu brengt kan men alleen maar omschrijven als: “insane”. Twee bassen, twee gitaristen, twee vocalisten, en een drummer. Velen beweren dat zanger en frontman Colin “onbereikbaar” is, maar deze avond in The Forum, Camden, London was Colin bereid al onze vragen te beantwoorden. Het interview vond plaats ter gelegenheid van 3 dagen Stig’s Gigs, een herinneringsfeest voor de Stig, de zanger van Icons Of Filth die enkele maanden geleden op een optreden stierf.

A: How did you get into Conflict – when did it start?

I was in another mess-about band before, called Splatterred Rockstars, and we started Conflict after seeing Crass basically.
Me and John started it, it was really wierd cause it just kinda came out of nothing. We were just lucky really, know what I mean?

A: Your logo means Anarcho-nihilist, doesn’t it?

Yeah, there’s two A’s in it standing for anarchy and autonomy, the N stand for Nihilism, but you can also read CND in it (CND = Campaign for Nuclear Disarmament, het grootste deel van de Britse vredesbeweging) , so people can see in it what they want. We invented it so it would have a striking image.

A: I always liked the line of Amebix in their song on Bullshit Detector 1 on Crass records: “I am anti-anarchist/ Nihilist?/ Yeah, that’s right, I am anti-nihilist.”
Where you immediately into the politics of punk or were you influenced by, say, The Clash or Crass?

Mainly The Clash, yeah, they showed me the sensible side to punk. We realised they had something sensible to say. I was into the punk-spirit before, but I realised, yeah, the Clash had something to say.

A: Personally I’m not a big Sex Pistols fan, but on your site you said they were one of your influences alongside Clash and Crass?

Yeah, that was mainly because the looks and the exitement of the time.

A: How did you meet Paco and Kevin?

We had another drummer before Paco but he wasn’t really interested to doing it, he just did it for fun basically and wanted to see Conflict big. Paco was with me at school, so I took him out of school to follow us.
Kevin, now dead, was our second guitarist. I’m actually doing a sort of Conflict family tree thing, because a lot of our members were in other good bands as well. When Kevin left (died?) we got Marshall and Ference doing the guitars. And Sarah with her red hair is now with us as a singer as well, so it’s growing.

A: And do Fear of Fear still exist, They had a site in the past but I never got hold of any merchandice?

Yeah they do, they do their own thing. As you know they were supposed to play here as well, but it didn’t happen.

(After the interview Paco told me he had given up playing for Fear of Fear but Sarah, John, Ferenc were still in it. Paco would only keep touring with Inner Terrestrials due to health-problems)

A: Which of your EP’s was the first, the one on Xntrix (Poison Girls related) or the one on Crass Records?

The Crass records one: “The house that man built”. Than “To a nation of animal lovers” on Corpus Christi” and then the Xntrix one: “Live at the centre Iberico.”

A: There was an insert with that one, it outlined your history, but I lost it, could your recapulate?

Was there an insert, Christ, I forgot, what did it say?

A: The only thing I remembered was that you played a lot with Flux of Pink Indians?

I’m sorry, I forgot.

A: Personally I liked the way Crass produced all these singles for these bands, but weren’t they all a little censored, maybe manipulated to a certain extent. I get this from an article by Josph Porta from the Mob (and Zounds, Null&Void, Blyth Power), where he wasn’t allowed to drum on the Zounds-single because “not good enough” and he also said he had no idea where the anarchist statements on the poster came from?

They (Crass) had a very certain way of how they wanted the covers to look and that’s fair enough.
But I wouldn’t say they censored anything. As for the music they had a very clear idea of how they wanted things to sound. They had a clear idea of how they wanted the production to sound. At the start I liked it very much.

A: Was there a difference with Corpus Christy?

It was just a sublabel of Crass-records really.

Or was there less control of Crass?

They would do things which they didn’t want to do on Crass-records. Like colour covers and things like that. You couldn’t do it on Crass, but you could do it on Corpus Christy.

A: And what made you do both the Mortarhate and Fight Back Records?

Mainly Mortarhate started as a tape label, just putting out little tapes. And then we put so much stuff out on different people’s labels that we learned how simple it was, just by doing it. We had learned how easy the process was.

A: And in what year did you get really famous, in the scene in London, and Britain, when you became bigger than, say, the other bands on Crass records?

That must have been round 1986 really. With the “Ungovernable Force’ album, that’s when it really went up.

A: And how was the scene than? Because I only got intereseted in it in 1980 and you were the first English band I ever saw.

It was strong, it was strong. I like the way it is now as well, with young and old faces together. But really the eighties were really good.

A: And what about all these clichés like glue-sniffing and so forth?

Most of that type didn’t come to see Conflict anymore by then because we weren’t doing any squat gigs anymore and moved to bigger halls.

A: Did you feel at that time, or any other time, that you had a “mission” with Conflict?

At that time, our main battle to be honest with you was with the police really. We had so much stick from them, we spend so much time, and they too, to counteract each other so there was nothing much we could achieve. We tried putting on gigs but were constantly harrassed.

A: They we’re really following you?

Yeah, all the time, stopping cars, giving little warning and so forth stuffed under your throat.

A: And were you officially banned from the UK?

We were served a paper, which was completely blank, and it had just to be filled in, but it never was properly served. (Ik vergat de naam van zulk formulier, AvV).

A: Did many agressive skinheads come to your gigs, and what was that story about your heavily wounded eye about?

Well, it first got damaged at a fight at a party, and afterwards the same eye was heavily hit again by skinheads, it was quite bad, but luckly it healed.

A: I’m personally intrigued about the lyrics of your song ‘Climbing the stairs’which deals with you being brutally attacked and nearly dead. The lyrics at the end go:
“Back alone in the shadow of a dead man,
I’m not alone, I’m reaching for the stair rails; instead I grab the hand of the father,
I see a face; it’s the face of my mother,
There’s someone else; it’s the mother of my mother
Whose strong man is helping me?
Climb up the stairs of the misery
Climb and climb the stairs from the mystery
Climbing the stairs for the unaware to see?”

Oh yeah, the ending about the grandfather and stuff. When I was young my granddad always took care of me and since he died, I’ve always had the feeling he was there for me, and he protected me and I was luckily never seriously harmed in a lot of bad incidents. That’s about it, in a nutshell.

A: What do you think of so many punks starting to eat meat again? Even people from Anti-sect, who produced such fine lyrics against animal abuse and the meat-industry.

It’s a shame really, but we’re not: “oh, we don’t wanna know you cos you eat meat”. They have to decide for themselves, there’s nothing we can do about it.

A: But aren’t you surprised, like I am?

Oh, definately I am surprised.

A: Paco, your drummer, always ate meat, was that because a decease, a food-allergy?

It’s true that he’s allergic to too many vegetables, but he’s Spanish, you know, and he choses to eat meat. He’s upstairs if you wanna chat with him.

A: Since Paco has serious health problems – cellulites – which one of the two drummers is gonna drum for Conflict in the future?

On recordings it’s gonna be both, but live it’s gonna be Nathan now. Because Paco can’t do the aircrafts anymore. A lot of the time we fly now, because it got so cheap and Paco’s not up to it anymore. His leg got so bad with that decease that we’re seriously worried about him. But he’s gonna keep playing for Inner Terrestrials, as long as they don’t go to big and he can do that.

A: I’m glad you’re so open to people as Joe Strummer (Clash, RIP) and that Justin of New Model Army despite the fact they signed to major labels, but there’s one band I can’t stand and that’s Chumbawamba. It’s not just that they signed to a major, but they still produce very long communiques with a rotten smell of old-fashioned communism – they’re playing it both ways?

There are more people who can’t stand them. But I just recently heared they did advertisement for a big American car-group, called BMW, and then donated the money they owed to protesters who were squatting BMW land, to preserve that part of nature. It surprired me when they signed to a major but I think their hearts are still in the right place, you know what I mean?

A: What are your hobbies or passions outside of Conflict?

I like to swim a lot. I really like that. Not much else outside of Conflict, I haven’t got the time.

A: Because many people think you are very healthy.

Yeah? I dunno (laughs).

A: You clearly give that expression. And art or books so?

No, I don’t have time for that, I’m always so busy with Conflict.

A: And in the nineties, when Conflict didn’t do much recording or gigging?

I went to clubs mainly and got into dance music, and I was also into open field parties. I loved that. Basically it was just hundreds of people going to a field and start raving.

A: On your CD-single ‘Carlo Giuliani’ you sing “This message commits those silent, to rise up, this time with violence ?”, and then ”Make sure the more they kill, the stronger our will.”. You put a question mark in after the word “violence” but I couldn’t hear it. Was that intentional? What is your take on it?

I put a question mark in, yeah, but we did that before with songs like “This is the A.L.F.”. If you put a question mark in, it’s not actually saying “DO IT!”, know what I mean.

A: So people can make from it what they like?

They can make their own assumption, yeah.

A: I didn’t buy your last CD because I already had the two CD-singles before, and I also saw your set yesterday, but the song “An option” is one a lot of people don’t like and I was wondering, would you, like The Xpozez once did, when dropping their song “Cocksucker” because their public took it be sexist, would you compromise and maybe stop playing it?

To me the song is written in a fashion that it’s not meant like a full out attack or something, there’s many ways you can look at that song and the way it develops. But I don’t like safety sometimes and I don’t write just that what people wanna hear and a lot of the scene, mainly in London is so politically correct that you can’t say anything, and that was probabably the reason for me to write it that way. But (laughter) I think the words were a bit strong, yeah.

A: Maybe that’s not bad. And what happened to your toe and wrist on your American tour?

I broke both on the stage in Los Angeles, when I fell and later let the microphone-standard drop on my feet, so that was a bit of a piss off.

A: I feel that after starting your former website and the release of ‘There must be another way’ another or a newer Conflict had appeared, and I like it very much. Now it’s five years on. Happy?

Yes. We’re feeling fresh again. Conflict is feeling fresh again. Certainly we’re very satisfied with the new album “There is no power without control”. And also the sound we’re making is a bit better.

A:This was basically it. Last questions: which are your short term and long term plans, and what do you think is the biggest achievement Conflict could ever make?

Cor… I don’t know really. The mid-term plan is we’re working on another album now. And to play as many places where we still can. I suppose there’s gonna come a time when we’re gonna try some different things, do some different festivals, you know what I mean. Without compromise really. But with Conflict we always had to take things bit by bit because you never know what’s round the corner with this band (laughs).

O yeah, in which two books were you mentioned as a “gang”?

One said that we weren’t in the underground really but that we were really uncontrolable, and mentioned our relation with the ALF and so on. The other one mentioned football etc. and said said we were thugs basically and very into violence. (laughs even louder).

O.K. that was it.
